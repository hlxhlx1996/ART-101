PHOTOGRAPHY   TECHNIQUES!


First, you will need to find a background clean enough to place your project. All kinds of filters or color modifications can be used in order to achieve the effect you want!

For this week, the material is even more abstract. While clay remains solid before and after it has been used for a crafting project, material for this week is still liquid when it was still a raw material. Material for this week is:


RESIN!


Initially, resin is used as an industrial material. Since it remains liquid until it has been mixed, and the final finish has great strength, it is often used to fix minor flaws of infrastructure. Although it has been called resin for the entire group, there are many kinds of resin for distinct uses. The one I used in the picture is the most common one: clear resin. The resin is transparent when there is no color added to it so the designers get to decide its finish. This kind of resin has two seperate portion and the user needes to pour a specific ratio of the two portions in order to let the resin dry properly. After mixing the two portions, adding colors, or other tiny stuff into the resin and pour them into the mold you wanted, it needes at least 24 hours to dry. Another kind of resin is called: UV resin. Instead of having to measure the amount of two portions of the resin, UV resin comes with only one bottle and is usually sold in different colors, either tranparent or opaque. UV resin won't dry in air, but will instead dry with UV light. UV resin is usually capable of making tiny crafts while the basic resin is used for larger crafts.

As I have already introduced several crafting materials, you may probably noticed that the materials are getting more and more abstract, which need more and more imaginations as well in order to make a great craft. Material for this week is even more abstract! While yarns will at least remain the texture after finishing the projects, material for this week is not even going to remain the original shape and even feel. Material for this week is:


CLAY!


There are four main types of clay that are mostly used for crafts, polymer clay, paper clay, airweight clay and bubber clay. For beginners, bubber clay is mostly used, since it won't dry for a long period of time. That is why it is also used for children. Airweight clay and paper clay both dries after putting them outside for a couple of hours. Airweight clay has smooth texture and vivid color, while paper clay has rigid texture and mostly white. Paper clay is mostly used for statue projects that may need rigid finish. For airweight clay, since it will alter its size a little after dried, it wont be suitable for detailed projects. This is where polymer clay comes into place. Polymer clay wont dry with air, but heat. Popular methods are baking or heat gun. The picture I showed on the left is two tiny polymer clay projects. You can see that the details can be better showed with polymer clay since it wont alter its size after drying and you can take your time to stick to the details since it wont dry before you bake it.

After spending four weeks to introduce blocks as materials of a craft, this week I will introduce a brand new material that requires even more technics and imagination.


YARNs!


There are two main types of techniques using yarns, knitting and crochet. For this week, I will introduce crochet. Unlike knitting, which is used mostly for sweathers, crochet can do more decorating crafts, such as dolls and blankets. It is fascinating to see how a ball of yarn can be turned into a doll! The picture shows a tiny doll I made with yarns using basic crochet technique. This type of crafts is called "Amigurumi", which is derived from Japanese meaning crocheting dolls. Although it may look pretty hard for someone who does not know how to knit, it is actually pretty simple and easy to catch up. While there may be a whole lot of different patterns of stiches, doing amigurumis only need one of them, the very basic single crochet. Variations only exits in amigurumi projects when there may be special decorations such as ears and teeth, or the technique on finishing the project. It spent me only one week to individually crochet a ball and finish a tiny bear, given that I dont have any knitting experience at all!

Since I have started to make fusing beads since I was freshman, and now I am a senior, I am really experienced with this material. Therefore I feel like I should spend two blogs to write about fusing beads. While I only made 2D projects when I first started this material. I soon got the idea that I can make 3D ones according to many fusing-beads project pictures. While the beads can only be fused as a board, you can always make three dimensional ones by making seperate parts to form a huge project.


The picture on the left is a puppy I made as one of my first three dimensional projects. There are two ways of building a three dimensional projects. For this week's blog, I will talk about something similar to LEGOs. This material also lets you to build blocks by yourself and realize your imagination. It is called:


FUSING BEADS!


Unlike LEGOs, fusing beads are not reusable. Once you get your project finished, you cannot get them back to beads again. The basic machanism in building projects with fusing beads is that you place your beads on a building board. When you are satisfied with your work, you need to put a paper above the beads and use an iron to heat the beads until both sides of them fused and merged into an entire board. The picture on the left is one project I did when I was just a beginner. Initially I planned to make a claw as a representation of nittany lion, but I did not get any blue beads in the starter kit. So I switched to make a pink one instead and put it on the wall. Even now I have several fusing beads project I made on my refrigerator! It is really satisfying to make everything you want with fusing beads, especially when you like anime.
